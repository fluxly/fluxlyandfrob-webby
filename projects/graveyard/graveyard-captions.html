Shempscape Contact Mic Preamp
A simple MOSFET-based circuit for impedence matching of piezo contact mics. Shempscape brand was intended as an umbrella for audio knick knacks and has recently been used in the Cryptozoologic app for the Shempscape Bytebeat Shufflebox synth.
Drawbot Controller
This is an Atmega-based 2-axis drawbot controller. Two Allegro stepper motor controllers with enable switches on the side. It can be used standalone or as a peripheral shield on a Raspberry Pi. It didn't make sense as a product but has been handy for one-off projects.
SMT Trippy Waves Clone
This is an Attiny-based version of Mitch Altman's Trippy Waves kit. The RGB LED goes through a pre-programmed sequence. There's an IR emitter and detector pointing up so when you wave your hand over the board it resets. USB header for power only.
Gesture Sensor Prototype
A breakout board for Microchip's MGC3030 gesture sensor (forgot to clean the flux from this one). This required a six inch diamneter sensing pad to be attached. I won't tell you the working title because I might use it on something else.
Bytebeat Shufflebox
Basically an Arduino with an audio jack. This is a variation on the Byteseeker kit and looks like it has 5 button inputs and at least one analog in. The corners are snap fit connectors that fit in the LEGO technics system.
Pot Swappers
A lot of the Modern Device microcontroller boards had a header for the analog pins to make it easier to use with breakout boards, since all analog sensing circuits also need power and ground. The header was compatible with servo cables that had a signal/power/ground pinout. The pot swapper remaps your potentiometer from ground/signal/power so it can be used with a servo-style cable (and also provides some LEGO technix-compatible holes).
Analog input breakout
I can't remember what this was for, but was probably for a bunch of potentiometer inputs. This board is made fab lab-style, milled on the Roland Modela, probably during Fab Academy.
Electret mic amp
This was a Fab Academy project, an elecret mic amp. This was a "Mondo" board, which came from "hello world" in Esperanto. It looks like the Mondo boards had a snowcone-style logo at the time as well.
Shempscape Mixer
Everyone uses those $90 Behringer mixers in art projects so the idea here was to make a simple and cheap 4 channel mixer.
Noisemusick Kit
The software version lives on but the original Noisemusick kit has been mothballed. The original kit was designed around two IR emitters that were wired backwards, so they leaked and acted as crude sensors. In recent years I've been unable to find IR emitters that are leaky enough; definitely time for a redesign!
Oh Eye With Audio Jack
This was a version of the Oh Eye board that tried to fix a problem with the Raspberry Pi's onboard analog audio output, which was very noisy. This provided an alternative jack from the outputs on the Pi's header pins, but required a driver update that ended up not working with the Pure Data patches that I was working on at the time.
Modkit Motor Driver
I think this was the first board with surface mount components I ever designed, hired by Ed Baafi for a Modkit prototype for a dual motor controller. In those days I was still stuck in the school of thought that you might swap out your H-bridge chips if you burned them out. I also forgot to do the Modkit logo as a bitmap of the actual logo. Sparkfun ended up doing this board in a more sensible way.
Reverbalizer Proto
This is the first working prototype of the Reverbalizer. I like how this shows you can use the fab lab to adapt a chip like the Spin FV-1 to fit a traditional breadboard.
Fluxamachine
This was just an expansion of the drawbot controller from 2 to 3 stepper drivers. Also has an ATX header for power.
RBBB for Raspberry Pi
One of Modern Device's first products was the $11 Really Bare Bones Board, an Arduino variant with the bare minimum of parts needed for a working project. This puts the RBBB on a Raspberry Pi shield. There's a prototyping section under the black breadboard, and this has logic level shifters so it'll work with 5V logic.
ESP32 prototype
Getting an ESP32 module on a breadboard with a milled breakout board and some blue wire.
Fluxamaboard
In 2010 we were getting Sanguino boards from Zach Hoeken at the Rep Rap Foundation and packaging them as kits. The Sanguino uses the Atmega644 so has twice as much memory as the bare bones Arduino (and more pins). The Fluxamaboard is just a surface mount module of the Sanguino kit.
